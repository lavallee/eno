# Systems Thinking

Systems thinking is the discipline of seeing wholes rather than isolated parts.
Instead of asking *what caused this*, it asks *what structure of relationships
keeps producing this*. A system is a set of elements interconnected in a way
that produces a characteristic pattern of behaviour over time.

## Stocks and flows

The two primitives are **stocks** (accumulations — water in a bathtub, trust in
a relationship, cash in an account) and **flows** (the rates that fill or drain
them). Most intuition failures come from reasoning about flows while ignoring the
stock they are changing. High [[Cognitive Load]] problems often hide a slow stock
quietly filling in the background.

## Feedback loops

Reinforcing loops amplify change; balancing loops resist it. Nearly every
counter-intuitive outcome — policy resistance, boom and bust, tragedy of the
commons — is a loop doing exactly what its structure dictates. Understanding the
loop is more useful than blaming the actors inside it.

## Leverage points

Small, well-placed changes can produce large effects. The highest leverage rarely
sits where attention naturally goes (parameters and numbers); it sits in the
rules, the goals, and the mindset that generated the system. This connects
closely to work in [[Mental Models and Decision Making]].
