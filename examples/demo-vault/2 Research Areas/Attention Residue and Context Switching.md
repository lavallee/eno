# Attention Residue and Context Switching

Attention residue is the sludge left behind when you switch tasks: a portion of your
focus stays stuck on the previous activity even after you have nominally moved on. The
result is that the first minutes of any new task are run at reduced capacity, paying
down the residue before real work can begin. In a day chopped into many small pieces,
that tax is paid over and over, and the pieces of genuine concentration between switches
shrink toward nothing.

## Why switching is so expensive

The cost is not the switch itself but the incomplete disengagement around it. When a
task is left unfinished — an email half-answered, a decision deferred — the mind keeps
a background process running on it, and that process competes for the same limited pool
of attention the new task needs. The effect is worse when the interrupted task was
demanding or unresolved, because unresolved problems are exactly the ones the mind
refuses to fully drop. Heavy [[Cognitive Load]] amplifies the residue, since there is
less spare capacity to absorb the overlap.

## The myth of multitasking

What is usually called multitasking is rapid task-switching, and each switch reloads a
fresh coat of residue. The subjective feeling of doing many things at once masks an
objective slowdown on all of them. Two demanding tasks interleaved take longer, and are
done worse, than the same two tasks run back to back. The feeling of productivity and
the fact of it point in opposite directions here, which is what makes the habit so hard
to break.

## Designing switches out

Because the cost lives in the switching, the remedy is to switch less. Batching similar
work, defending long unbroken blocks, and finishing tasks to a clean stopping point
before moving on all reduce the residue carried forward. A deliberately closed loop —
writing down exactly where you will resume — lets the background process quiet down,
because the mind trusts that the unfinished thread has been captured somewhere safe.
Reaching a [[Flow State]] is largely a matter of stringing together enough
uninterrupted time that the residue finally clears and full capacity comes online.

## The compounding cost

The damage is not linear. A single interruption in an hour is an annoyance; an
interruption every few minutes prevents the deeper modes of thought from ever
starting, because they require a runway the fragmented day never provides. Over weeks,
a working style built on constant switching quietly caps the difficulty of the problems
a person can take on — not through any lack of ability, but because the hardest thinking
simply never gets the continuous stretch it needs to happen at all.
