# Information Foraging Theory

Information foraging theory borrows a striking idea from ecology: people looking for
information behave a lot like animals looking for food. Both are trying to maximise
valuable finds per unit of effort, and both are constantly, half-consciously
deciding whether to keep exploiting the patch they are in or move on to a richer one
somewhere else. Reframing search and reading as *foraging* turns a fuzzy activity
into something with a shape you can reason about.

## Patches, scent, and diet

Three concepts do most of the work. A **patch** is any local pool of information — a
document, a folder, a search-results page, a single promising thread. **Information
scent** is the set of cues that hint at how much value a patch holds before you have
paid the cost of entering it: a link's wording, a heading, a snippet, the shape of a
result. **Diet** is the set of things a forager chooses to pursue given the scent
available and the cost of getting there.

The core prediction is a rate-maximising rule. A forager stays in a patch while the
expected value of continuing there exceeds the expected value of leaving to find a
better one, accounting for the travel cost of switching. When a patch is rich, you
dig in. When its returns dwindle below what you could get elsewhere, you leave — and
good foragers leave *before* a patch is fully exhausted, because the last scraps are
rarely worth the effort.

## Why scent is everything

If scent is strong and honest, foraging is efficient: cues point reliably toward
value and the forager rarely wastes effort on dead ends. If scent is weak or
misleading, the forager thrashes — opening and abandoning patches, backtracking,
and burning attention on navigation rather than on the information itself. Much of
what we call good design for information systems is really the engineering of
trustworthy scent, so that a person can predict a patch's value cheaply and be right
often enough to keep moving.

This is also why a badly labelled note is worse than no note. A weak title emits weak
scent; the note will not be found by the future forager who needs it, and the effort
that produced it is silently wasted. Naming is not decoration — it is the scent trail
your later self follows.

## Applying it to your own vault

Treat your knowledge base as a landscape of patches and ask where the scent is weak.
Vague titles, orphaned notes with no trail leading in, and undifferentiated dumps of
clippings are all low-scent patches — technically present, practically invisible.
Strengthening scent means writing titles that state a claim, linking notes so that
trails lead into them, and pruning patches that no longer repay a visit.

Foraging theory also explains why frictionless capture can backfire. Every saved item
is a new patch competing for future attention; save indiscriminately and you flood
your own landscape with low-value patches, weakening the average scent and making the
genuinely valuable material harder to forage. A smaller, better-tended set of patches
is easier to hunt than a vast, undifferentiated one.

## The discipline it rewards

The forager's mindset is a useful corrective to completism. You will never read
everything, and trying to is a foraging strategy that maximises effort rather than
value. The skill is to read the scent well, commit quickly to rich patches, abandon
poor ones without guilt, and keep the whole landscape legible enough that your future
self can forage it too.
