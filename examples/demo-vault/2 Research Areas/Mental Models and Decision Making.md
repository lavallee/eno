# Mental Models and Decision Making

A mental model is a compressed representation of how some part of the world works —
a reusable pattern you can carry from one situation to the next. We reason with
models whether or not we notice them; the only choice is whether they are good ones,
consciously held, or bad ones running silently in the background.

## A latticework, not a list

Collecting isolated tips produces brittle knowledge. The more useful goal is a
*latticework* of models drawn from many disciplines — feedback and equilibrium from
[[Systems  Thinking]], opportunity cost from economics, base rates from statistics,
selection pressure from biology. A problem viewed through several models at once is
far harder to fool you than a problem viewed through one.

## Common failure modes

Most decision errors are not failures of intelligence but of perspective. We anchor
on the first number we hear, over-weight vivid stories against dull base rates, and
mistake confidence for accuracy. Naming these patterns is half the defence: a bias
you can point at is a bias you can partially correct.

## Deciding under uncertainty

Good decisions are judged by process, not only outcome — a sound bet can lose and a
reckless one can win. The habit worth building is to reason in probabilities, seek
disconfirming evidence, and treat being wrong as information rather than insult. This
is where [[Deliberate Practice]] applies to thinking itself: you improve judgement by
making calls, checking them, and adjusting the models that produced them.
